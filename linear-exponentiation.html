Linear Exponentiation

A different kind of exponentiation — shell decomposition for linear thinkers

An = A + (A³ − A) × ((A(n−1) − 1)/(A² − 1))

Patrick's insight: Exponentiation doesn't have to be multiplicative. This formula reconstructs An as a linear accumulation — the base A plus a series of "shells" that expand according to geometric principles.

The key insight is that (A³ − A) factors as (A−1)·A·(A+1) — the product of three consecutive integers. This "shell" becomes the fundamental tile of the exponential structure. The fraction (A^(n−1) − 1)/(A² − 1) is the sum of a geometric series that tells us how many such tiles fit in the nth power.

This bridges geometric and linear thinking: each base A produces a unique triangulation pattern, and the exponential power is measured in units of that base's shell.

🔢 The Mathematics

Derivation

The geometric series identity:

(A(n−1) − 1)/(A² − 1) = 1 + A² + A⁴ + ... + A(n−3) (for odd n)

This sum represents the accumulation of all lower even powers. When multiplied by the shell (A³−A) and added to the base A, we reconstruct An.

Key Properties

  • The shell (A³−A) = (A−1)A(A+1) is always divisible by 6
  • For A=2, the shell is 6 — the same "six" that appears in the 2+6=8 pattern
  • The geometric factor approaches A(n−3) as n grows large
  • This formula converts multiplicative growth into additive accumulation

🔗 Connection to Proportional Mathematics

This linear exponentiation formula is the mathematical foundation of Patrick's Proportional Mathematics framework. It shows that:

  • Exponentiation is measurable — in units of the shell (A³−A)
  • Growth is triangulation — each base produces a unique geometric pattern
  • The unit = base/tile — consistent proportional scaling
  • Computation is analog — exponentiation maps to physical accumulation

This bridges the gap between geometric thinking (multiplicative, exponential) and linear thinking (additive, sequential). For those who struggle to visualize An as repeated multiplication, this formula shows it as the base plus some number of shell units — a linear accumulation that happens to equal the exponential power.

🔧 The Engine Exposed

Patrick's alternative formulation exposes the recursion directly — the "engine open and running." This shows the iterative construction that builds the geometric factor step by step:

An >< x₀=0
(n−1) × [ x := Ax + u ]
      ⇒ A + (A³−A)x

Where u = 1/(A²−1). The symbol >< indicates "construct target from initial state."

How It Works

  1. Start with x₀ = 0 (the "current proportion")
  2. Loop (n−1) times, each iteration updating x := Ax + u
  3. After the loop, An = A + (A³−A)·x

Why This Matters

The closed formula An = A + (A³−A)((A(n−1)−1)/(A²−1)) gives the answer in one step. But the iterative form shows the process — how the geometric series accumulates through repeated application of the same simple rule. Each iteration adds another shell-sized contribution, building the exponential through linear accumulation.

This is the "relative line" view: a single recursive process without the "tile" (the closed-form geometric series) in the way. For understanding how the system breathes, the engine view is often more illuminating than the blueprint.
